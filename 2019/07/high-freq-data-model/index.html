Notes on High Frequency Data Model

Jul 26, 2019

Hypothesis Test and Confidence Interval

A formal statistical data analysis includes:

  • hypothesis testings: seeing whether a structure is present
  • confidence interval: setting error limits on estimates
  • prediction interval: setting error limits on future outcomes

We will discuss a typical setting as follow:

  • Let $\beta$ be the unknown parameter, and $\hat{\beta}_n$ is the parameter estimator based on $n$ observations
  • Let $\hat{\sigma}^2_n$ be the estimator of $\sigma^2_n$, equal to $var(\sqrt{n}(\hat{\beta}_n - \beta))$
  • In rare circumstances where $\sqrt{n}(\hat{\beta}_n - \beta)/\hat{\sigma}$ is independent of the unknown $\beta$, such as in regression settings where the error terms $\epsilon_i$ are i.i.d. normally distributed, we can show that it follows a t-distribution with $n-1$ degree of freedom.

$$ \dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{\beta}n - \beta)}{\hat{\sigma}} \sim t{n-1} $$

  • However, asymptotically as $n\rightarrow\infty$, the Central Limit Theorem applies which release us from the assumption restriction above. Under a variety of regular conditions:

$$ \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty} \dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{\beta}_n - \beta)}{\hat{\sigma}} \sim \mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$

  • The $1-\alpha$ confidence interval can then be computed as:

$$ \beta \in CI = \hat{\beta}n \pm t^{\alpha}{n-1}\hat{\sigma}n/\sqrt{n} \ \text{or asymptotically, swapping } t^{\alpha}{n-1} \text{ by } z^{\alpha} $$

  • The $1-\alpha$ hypothesis test of $\beta=\beta_0$ would:

    • accept $H_0$ that $\beta=\beta_0$ if $\beta \in CI$
    • reject $H_0$ that $\beta=\beta_0$ if $\beta \notin CI$

  • The p-value is $\alpha$ such that the hypothesis test is indifference between accept or reject.

  • In practice, t-distribution is typically used for regression data as it is more conservative ($t^{\alpha}_{n-1} > z^{\alpha}$). For non-regression data, normal distribution is often used.

Some background on LLN and CLT:

  • Given $n$ i.i.d. random variable $X_i$. Let $\bar{X} = \sum X_i/n$

  • The Law of Large Numbers states that if $\mathbb{E}|X|<\infty$, then $\lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\bar{X}\rightarrow\mathbb{E}X$ a.s.

  • The Central Limit Theorem states that if $\mathbb{E}X^2<\infty$, then

$$\begin{align} \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\sqrt{n}(\bar{X}-\mathbb{E}X)&\rightarrow\mathcal{N}(0, var[X]) \ \text{or equivalently, } \lim_{n\rightarrow\infty}\dfrac{\bar{X}-\mathbb{E}X}{var[\bar{X}]}&\rightarrow\mathcal{N}(0, 1) \end{align}$$

  • Therefore, if we have a consistent estimator $\hat{\sigma}_n$ for $\sigma_n$, meaning $\hat{\sigma}_n\rightarrow\sigma$ as $n\rightarrow\infty$, Slutsky's Theorem concludes that

$$ \dfrac{\sqrt{n}(\hat{\beta}_n - \beta)}{\hat{\sigma}_n} \rightarrow\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$$

  • We can then use the normal distribution to set confidence intervals and perform hypothesis tests.

A High Frequency Data Model

Assume that $X_t = \log{S_t}$ follows Geometric Brownian motion.

$$X_t = X_0 + \mu t + \sigma W_t$$

Parametric Estimator

Assume equal spacing between $n$ time steps, $\Delta t = T/n$ and $t_i = i\Delta t$. Given observations $X_{t_i}$, the step $\Delta X_{t_{i+1}} = X_{t_{i+1}} - X_{t_i}$ and $\Delta X_{t_{t+1}} \sim \mathcal{N}(\mu\Delta t, \sigma^2\Delta t)$

We have the following parametric estimator: $$\begin{align} \hat{\mu} &= \dfrac{1}{n\Delta t}\sum_0^{n-1}\Delta X_{t_{i+1}} = \dfrac{X_T - X_0}{T} \ \hat{\sigma}^2_{MLE} &= \dfrac{1}{n\Delta t}\sum_0^{n-1}(\Delta X_{t_{i+1}} - \overline{\Delta X}{t})^2 \ \hat{\sigma}^2{UMVUE} &= \dfrac{1}{(n-1)\Delta t}\sum_0^{n-1}(\Delta X_{t_{i+1}} - \overline{\Delta X}_{t})^2 \end{align}$$

Observation $\hat{\mu}$

  • satisfies both MLE and UMVUE
  • For $T<\infty$, can NOT consistently estimate $\mu$, as $\hat{\mu}$ does not depend on $n$
  • For $T\rightarrow\infty$, can consistently estimate $\mu$, as $var(\hat{\mu}) = \sigma^2/T \rightarrow 0$ and therefore $\hat{\mu} \rightarrow \mu$ as $T\rightarrow\infty$

Observation $\hat{\sigma}^2_{MLE}$, $\hat{\sigma}^2_{UMVUE}$

  • For $T<\infty$, $\hat{\sigma}^2$ can consistently estimate $\sigma^2$
  • Let $U_i = \Delta X_{t_i}/(\sigma\Delta t^{1/2})$, then $U_i$ are i.i.d normal distribution $\mathcal{N}(\mu\Delta t^{1/2}/\sigma, 1)$. Let $\bar{U} = \dfrac{1}{n}\sum_0^{n-1}U_i $, then,

$$ \sum_0^{n-1} (U_i - \bar{U})^2 \sim \chi_{n-1}^2 $$

  • For the Uniform Minimum Variance Unbiased (UMVUE) estimator,

$$\begin{align} \hat{\sigma}^2_{UMVUE} &= \dfrac{1}{(n-1)\Delta t}\sum_0^{n-1}(\Delta X_{t_{i+1}} - \overline{\Delta X}{t})^2 \ &= \dfrac{\sigma^2\Delta t}{(n-1)\Delta t}\sum_0^{n-1}(U{t_{i}} - \bar{U}{t})^2 \ &\sim \dfrac{\sigma^2}{n-1}\chi^2{n-1} \end{align}$$

  • Since $\mathbb{E}\chi^2_{n-1} = n-1$ and $var(\chi^2_{n-1}) = 2(n-1)$. Therefore $\hat{\sigma}^2$ consistently estimates $\sigma^2$,

$$\mathbb{E}(\hat{\sigma}^2_{UMVUE}) = \sigma^2 \ var(\hat{\sigma}^2_{UMVUE}) = \dfrac{2\sigma^4}{n-1}$$

  • Since $\chi^2_{n-1}$ is the sum of $n-1$ i.i.d. $\chi^2_1$, by CLT we have $[\chi^2_{n-1}-(n-1)]/\sqrt{2(n-1)}\sim\mathcal{N}(0, 1)$. Therefore,

$$\begin{align} n^{1/2}(\hat{\sigma}^2-\sigma^2) &\sim (n-1)^{1/2} (\hat{\sigma}^2-\sigma^2) \ &\rightarrow \sqrt{2}\sigma^2 [\chi^2_{n-1}-(n-1)]/\sqrt{2(n-1)} \ &\sim \mathcal{N}(0, 2\sigma^4) \end{align}$$

  • Therefore, an asymptotic 95% confidence interval for $\sigma^2$ can be formulated as:

$$ \sigma^2 = \hat{\sigma}^2 \pm 1.96 \times \sqrt{2}\hat{\sigma}^2/\sqrt{n} $$

  • Since $\hat{\sigma}^2_{MLE}=\hat{\sigma}^2_{UMVUE}(n-1)/n$, the same asymptotical confidence interval applies

Non-parametric Estimator

We first define the integrated volatility (IV):

$$\begin{align} IV &= \langle X, X\rangle_T \ &= \int_0^T \sigma^2_tdt \ &= \lim_{\Delta t\rightarrow\infty} \sum (X_{t_{i+1}} - X_{t_i})^2 \end{align}$$

In theory, IV of high frequency data can be estimated accurately with the realized volatility estimator $[X, X]_ T = \sum (X_{t_{i+1}} - X_{t_i})^2 $. This estimator is consistent and asymptotically mixed normal with variance equal to $2T/n \times \int_0^T\sigma^4dt$ and estimated by $2/3 \times [X, X, X, X]_ T$

In practice, $[X, X]_ T$ does not converge as $\Delta t\rightarrow 0$. Possible reason due to the existance of microstructure, e.g. bid-ask spread, limited market depth, price impact, strategic trading.
